The History

Founded on principle.
Built on precedent.

Harlow & Voss LLP was founded in the winter of 1961 in a three-room office on the fourteenth floor of 30 Broad Street — a building that smelled of old paper and ambition. Its two principals arrived from opposite ends of the American legal world, drawn together by a shared conviction: that the law, practiced without fear or deference, was the most powerful instrument a civilization had ever produced.

The firm's first decade was defined by scarcity and ferocity in equal measure. Edmund Harlow and Pieter Voss took every case the white-shoe firms declined — antitrust battles no one thought winnable, criminal defenses for clients whose innocence was commercially inconvenient, corporate disputes where the opposing counsel outnumbered them eight to one. They won more than they lost. They lost less than anyone expected. By 1970, they had twenty-two attorneys and a waiting list.

The move to One Vanderbilt — the soaring obsidian tower above Grand Central Terminal — came in 2021, crowning a firm that had long outgrown its origins. Floors 45 through 47 are now the firm's domain: 47,000 square feet of black granite, floor-to-ceiling glass overlooking Midtown, and the particular silence of a place where time is billed at rates that demand it.

1961
Harlow & Voss opens at 30 Broad Street. Two partners, one paralegal, one secretary. First retainer: $8,000 from a garment manufacturer facing federal trade violations. The case is won in eleven days.
1967
The firm's first landmark win: Kellner Industries v. the United States — a federal antitrust case that rewrites how vertical integration is assessed under the Sherman Act. Pieter Voss argues before the Second Circuit for six uninterrupted hours. The opinion cites his brief seventeen times.
1974
The firm moves to Park Avenue. First expansion: the White Collar Criminal Defense division is formally established, drawing attorneys who have clerked at the Supreme Court level. The firm reaches fifty attorneys for the first time.
1981
Edmund Harlow successfully defends Aldus-Croft Financial in what the New York Times calls "the securities fraud trial of the decade." Harlow's closing argument is studied at Columbia Law School for the next two decades.
1993
Pieter Voss retires from active practice. The Voss Chair in Litigation Excellence is endowed at Harvard Law School in his name. He remains Senior Counsel Emeritus until his death in 2009.
2003
Edmund Harlow steps back from trial work. The firm's first managing partner from outside the founding generation — Catherine Aldredge, Columbia '84 — assumes leadership. Harlow dies in 2011, leaving the firm $40 million in endowment and a forty-two-page letter on the ethics of advocacy.
2021
The firm relocates to One Vanderbilt, floors 45–47. 47,000 square feet. The war room on the 47th floor becomes the most feared conference room in Manhattan litigation.
Today
Harlow & Voss employs 47 associates, 19 partners, and a support staff of over two hundred. The firm has never lost a jury trial at the federal level. This fact is stated without qualification in every recruiting document the firm produces. It has never been challenged.

Practice Divisions

Three disciplines.
One standard.

From its earliest years, the firm resisted the instinct to diversify broadly. Where other firms expanded into real estate, family law, and insurance work, Harlow and Voss made a deliberate choice: depth over breadth. The firm practices in three areas, each considered by its own peers to be the most elite expression of that discipline in the Eastern United States.

I
Corporate Litigation
The firm's founding discipline and still its largest. Harlow & Voss handles disputes between entities whose names are on exchange tickers and in quarterly filings: mergers challenged mid-close, shareholder derivative actions, breach of fiduciary duty at the board level, and the quiet war of contract interpretation where eight words in a term sheet determine eight-figure outcomes. The division operates on a minimum threshold of $50 million in dispute value. Below that figure, the matter is declined. This has been firm policy since 1974.
Minimum $50M Threshold
II
White Collar Criminal Defense
Established in 1974 after Pieter Voss successfully defended a senior Treasury official against federal corruption charges in a case the Washington Post described as "unwinnable." The division represents CEOs, board members, hedge fund principals, and government officials facing federal prosecution. The attorneys here are former SDNY prosecutors, former public defenders of extraordinary talent, and clerks who have sat beside federal judges and understand precisely how they think. When the Southern District of New York indicts your client, this is where you come.
Federal Jurisdiction
III
Institutional Crisis Management
The division that does not appear in press releases. Formally it handles reputational litigation, pre-litigation advisory, regulatory negotiation, and the suppression of legally actionable disclosures before they surface in discovery or the press. Informally it is where Manhattan's most powerful institutions send their most sensitive problems. The attorneys here do not appear in court. They appear in rooms where the outcome is determined before a filing is ever made. The division was Edmund Harlow's final creation, established in 1998, and was never discussed publicly in his lifetime.

The Record

Cases that became
the law itself.

Harlow & Voss does not publish a case list. The following is a partial account drawn from public court records, judicial opinions, and the firm's own institutional memory — that precise, uncelebrated knowledge of what was decided, and by whom, and at what cost.

1967
Kellner Industries v. United States — Second Circuit opinion rewrites vertical integration doctrine under the Sherman Act. Voss's brief becomes mandatory reading at seven law schools within a decade.
1978
Renfrew Capital, SEC Investigation — Harlow negotiates a consent decree that preserves the firm's trading operations entirely. The SEC's enforcement chief later describes the negotiation as "the most precise legal dismantling I have witnessed from the opposing side."
1981
United States v. Aldus-Croft Financial — Harlow's closing argument in the federal securities fraud trial of the decade. Acquittal on all twelve counts. The jury deliberates for forty minutes.
1989
Morrow-Lindqvist Merger Arbitration — The firm's first nine-figure arbitration. A disputed $1.1 billion merger acquisition. The award is delivered in the firm's favor in its entirety. The dissenting arbitrator's opinion is two sentences long.
1997
In re: Sentinel Group Holdings — White Collar division secures dismissal of a seventy-three-count federal indictment on procedural grounds. The SDNY does not re-indict. The case is studied in federal criminal procedure courses as a masterwork of pre-trial motion strategy.
2008
Vantage-Meridian Financial Crisis Litigation — During the market collapse, the firm simultaneously represents three separate institutional clients in interconnected derivative litigation. All three matters resolve favorably. No conflict of interest is ever established.
2015
Harlow & Voss v. New York Bar Association — In a rare instance of the firm litigating on its own behalf, the firm successfully challenges an ethics inquiry targeting one of its Crisis Management partners. The Bar Association drops the matter. The opinion is sealed.
2022
Kreuz Capital Antitrust Defense — The firm's most recent landmark matter. A $4.2 billion antitrust action brought by the DOJ. The case is dismissed at summary judgment. The opinion runs to 187 pages and cites Voss's 1967 brief in its reasoning — fifty-five years later.
The Founders

Two men.
One argument that lasted sixty years.

Edmund Harlow and Pieter Voss disagreed on nearly everything except the law. Their partnership endured for over three decades on the shared conviction that the law, rigorously applied without deference to wealth or power, was the only honest architecture a civilization could build. They were wrong about each other constantly and right about the law almost always. The firm they created outlasted both of them.

Edmund J. Harlow
1924 — 2011
Founding Partner · Corporate & Criminal Litigation

Born in New Haven, Connecticut, the son of a customs judge and a schoolteacher. Educated at Phillips Andover and then Yale College, where he read history and finished first in his class. Yale Law followed — he graduated in 1949, clerked for Justice Felix Frankfurter on the Supreme Court, and then, in a decision that baffled everyone who knew him, declined three offers from the most prestigious firms in New York and began building his own.

Harlow was the firm's public face: courtroom-oriented, rhetorically gifted, possessed of the kind of patrician calm that juries instinctively trusted. He never raised his voice in a courtroom. He never needed to. His cross-examinations were described by opposing counsel as "an education in how terribly wrong a witness can be about their own recollection." He tried 63 cases to verdict in his career. He lost four.

"The law does not care about the truth. The law cares about what can be proven, in the right room, to the right people, at the right moment. Our job is to control all three variables."
— Edmund Harlow, Columbia Law School address, 1984
&
Pieter H. Voss
1921 — 2009
Founding Partner · Federal Litigation & Appellate

Born in Rotterdam, the Netherlands. He survived the German occupation as a teenager and emigrated to the United States in 1946 with eleven dollars and a letter of introduction to a Columbia law professor who had heard about him through a mutual friend in The Hague. He earned his law degree in two and a half years. He spoke with a Dutch accent until the day he died and considered it an advantage in the courtroom: no one, he said, ever assumed they understood him.

Where Harlow persuaded, Voss dismantled. His specialty was the appellate brief — documents of such structural precision and argumentative density that federal judges reportedly read them twice before responding. His reputation for courtroom brutality was not exaggerated: he once cross-examined a hostile expert witness for nine consecutive hours, never repeating a question, and reduced the man's entire testimony to rubble without raising his voice above a conversational register.

"I am not interested in winning. Winning is a consequence. I am interested in being so correct that losing becomes structurally impossible."
— Pieter Voss, Harvard Law Review interview, 1979
The Legacy
"There are firms that represent power. There are firms that resist it. Harlow & Voss, at its best, did something rarer: it made power answerable."
— The American Lawyer, Profile of the Firm, 2001

The firm's founding partners left behind more than a legal practice. They left a doctrine — an internally transmitted understanding of what it means to practice law without compromise — that has shaped every attorney who has passed through these offices. The names change. The floors change. The rate sheet changes. The standard does not.
